\noindent\textbf{Question 1: A science table}

\medskip
\noindent A study population has four units. The full ``science table'' (normally unobservable) is:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{c c c c c}
  $i$ & $D_i$ & $Y_i(1)$ & $Y_i(0)$ & $\tau_i$ \\ \hline
  1 & 1 & 8 & 3 & \\
  2 & 1 & 4 & 5 & \\
  3 & 0 & 6 & 2 & \\
  4 & 0 & 3 & 3 & \\
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item Fill in $\tau_i$. Is the treatment effect the same for every unit?

\vspace{1.2cm}

\item Compute the ATE, the ATT, and the ATC.

\vspace{2.5cm}

\item Which cells of this table would you \emph{actually} get to observe in a real study? Cross out the ones you would not.

\vspace{1cm}

\item Compute the naive difference in means, $\E[Y_i \mid D_i=1] - \E[Y_i \mid D_i=0]$, using only the observed cells. Compare it to the ATT. Are they the same?

\vspace{2.5cm}
\end{enumerate}

\clearpage

%%% QUESTION 2 %%%
\noindent\textbf{Question 2: Decomposing the difference in means}

\medskip
\noindent Show (a few lines of algebra, using consistency) that
\[
\E[Y_i \mid D_i=1] - \E[Y_i \mid D_i=0]
\;=\;
\underbrace{\E[Y_i(1)-Y_i(0) \mid D_i=1]}_{\text{ATT}}
\;+\;
\underbrace{\E[Y_i(0) \mid D_i=1] - \E[Y_i(0) \mid D_i=0]}_{\text{baseline bias}}.
\]

\vspace{4.5cm}

\noindent Verify the decomposition numerically on the science table from Question 1.

\vspace{3cm}

\noindent In plain English, what does the ``baseline bias'' term represent? Under what assumption about $D$ does it vanish?
